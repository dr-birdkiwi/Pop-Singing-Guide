# How to Use This Guide

This is a reference document, not a course. It's organized by topic so you can jump to whatever section is relevant to where you are right now. Some sections will make immediate sense; others will only click after weeks or months of practice. That's normal — return to them.

A few principles to keep in mind throughout:

**Anatomy is for diagnosis, not real-time control.** Knowing that a high belt involves a particular muscular coordination helps you understand why certain approaches fail and others work. It does *not* help you sing the note in real time. Singing is an automated motor skill driven by sound goals, vowel sensations, and emotional intention — not by conscious muscle commands. Use the anatomy in this guide to choose your exercises and diagnose what went wrong, not to think your way through a phrase mid-performance.

**Sound goals over muscle goals.** When practicing, aim for a target sound or sensation, not a target muscle action. "Brighter," "more buzz here," "like calling out to a friend" — these cues work. "Engage your cricothyroid more" does not.

**Most rules have counter-rules.** Pop technique is a system of conditional coordinations. Vowels narrow *or* widen depending on the mix you want. Vibrato is on *or* off depending on the phrase. Pressure is high *or* near-zero depending on whether you're belting or using vocal fry. The intermediate singer learns rules; the advanced singer learns the conditions under which each rule flips. This guide tries to teach both layers.

**Scope.** This guide addresses contemporary Anglo-American pop, R&B, and power-pop belt traditions. K-pop, Latin pop, country, traditional musical theatre, jazz, and indie/folk-pop overlap with this material but use distinct production strategies in places. If you cross into those genres, some advice here will need adjustment.

**Your teacher is the primary source; this guide is a companion.** Written words can describe what's happening and why — they cannot hear you, watch your jaw, or feel your tension. Throughout this guide, I'll flag the things that genuinely need a teacher's ears and the things you can productively work on alone.
