# Appendix: Glossary

**Anchoring** — Muscular stability across torso, neck, and articulators that supports the voice during powerful or sustained singing.

**Aryepiglottic sphincter (epilaryngeal tube)** — Small space just above the vocal folds. Narrowing it produces twang.

**Belt** — A loud, dense, chest-like vocal quality used at high pitches in pop and musical theatre. For most singers, a high belt is a head-mix (CT-dominant) acoustically disguised as chest voice.

**Break** — The audible flip between chest voice and head voice that occurs at the upper passaggio in untrained voices. Smoothed by developing mix.

**Cardioid** — Microphone polar pattern that picks up sound from the front and rejects sound from behind. Most live mics are cardioid.

**Chest voice** — Lower, weightier, speech-like vocal register. TA-dominant. Sympathetic vibration felt in chest and throat.

**Cricothyroid (CT)** — Muscle that stretches the vocal folds longer and thinner. CT-dominant coordination produces head voice.

**Falsetto** — Lighter, often breathier upper register, especially in male voices. Can overlap with head voice.

**Formant** — A resonance peak in the vocal tract. Vowels are differentiated by their formant patterns.

**Head voice** — Higher, lighter vocal register. CT-dominant. Sympathetic vibration felt in face and head.

**Larynx** — The voice box, containing the vocal folds.

**Mix** — Coordination that blends features of chest and head voice, allowing smooth ascent through the passaggio. Can be chest-dominant, balanced, or head-dominant.

**Onset** — How a note begins. Types include balanced, breathy, glottal, and creaky/fry.

**Passaggio** — Italian for "passage." Transition zone between vocal registers. The upper passaggio (between chest and head voice) is the most practically important for pop singers.

**Phonation** — Sound production by the vocal folds.

**Proximity effect** — Bass boost that occurs when singing close to a cardioid microphone.

**Resonance** — The amplification and shaping of vocal-fold vibration by the vocal tract.

**Singer's formant** — A clustering of upper formants in classically trained voices that allows acoustic projection. Pop ring overlaps with but isn't identical to this.

**Subglottal pressure** — Air pressure below the vocal folds. High in belt; low in fry.

**Support** — Coordinated muscular work in the torso that controls breath release for singing.

**Thyroarytenoid (TA)** — Muscle that thickens and shortens the vocal folds. TA-dominant coordination produces chest voice.

**Tessitura** — The range where a voice sits most comfortably, distinct from total range.

**Twang** — Narrowing of the aryepiglottic sphincter, producing high-frequency spectral boost. The "ring" or "edge" in pop voices.

**Vibrato** — Pitch oscillation in a sustained note, typically 5–7 Hz when healthy.

**Vocal fold (vocal cord)** — Two folds of tissue in the larynx that vibrate to produce sound.

**Vocal fry** — Low, crackly register. Loose thick folds at very low pressure.

**Vocal tract** — The pharynx, mouth, and nasal cavity above the vocal folds. Shapes the raw fold vibration into the audible voice.

**Whistle register** — Highest vocal register, above head voice. Used by some sopranos for very high notes.
